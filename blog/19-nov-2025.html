An Inside Look Into India’s POS & Biometric Devices Boom

Given the volume and speed of growth, selecting the right POS/biometric hardware is no longer just about devices, it’s about reliability, future-readiness, versatility, and local support.

India’s payment and retail infrastructure is evolving fast. As digital payments, UPI, and hybrid payment modes expand, the demand for robust, locally-deployable POS and biometric devices has surged, creating an opportunity for manufacturers and vendors who understand Indian needs.

For procurement teams, system integrators, and retail / service-sector buyers, this shift means: design for scale, focus on compliance, and pick vendors who build for real Indian conditions. That’s where Access Computech aims to stand out.

POS Devices are on a steady upward trajectory

To understand where India’s POS ecosystem is heading, it helps to step back and look at the market forces shaping it. Over the past five years, India has transformed from a cash-heavy retail environment to a hybrid payments economy, and device deployments have followed the money.

India’s POS device market, which includes fixed countertop terminals, mobile POS, Android-based smart POS, and cloud-integrated systems, stood at US $536.44 million in 2024. The growth outlook is even more striking: the market is projected to reach US $1,362.54 million by 2033, expanding at a CAGR of 10.37% (2025–2033).

(Data Source: IMARC Group)

A set of concrete behavioural and infrastructural shifts are driving this growth:

1. The UPI effect

UPI’s rise hasn’t eliminated the need for POS devices. Ironically, it has strengthened it. Today’s consumer expects multiple payment rails at every checkout: cards, UPI, tap-to-pay, QR, wallets.

Worldline’s payments report highlights that QR + UPI now complements card-based transactions rather than replacing them, pushing demand for versatile terminals rather than single-mode devices.

2. SMEs are digitising faster than ever before

The fastest growth is no longer coming from malls or chain stores, it’s coming from small businesses.

From neighborhood kiranas to independent pharmacies to Tier-3 retail clusters, SMEs are adopting POS terminals to track sales, reduce cash-handling risk, and accept hybrid payments.

As per IMARC’s analysis (as shared earlier in this article), SMEs and service-sector businesses account for a rising share of POS terminal deployments, a trend expected to intensify as GST compliance, inventory integration, and digital billing become standard practice.

3. Expansion beyond metro India

Earlier, POS deployments were largely localised in urban centers. That concentration is disappearing.

Retail growth in Tier-2 and Tier-3 cities, coupled with aggressive network expansion by banks and fintechs, has significantly broadened the geographic footprint of POS installations.

As a result, demand has shifted towards:

  • rugged terminals suited for semi-urban and rural conditions
  • portable Android POS devices for delivery-based businesses
  • battery-efficient mobile POS for fuel stations, logistics, and transport
4. The rise of smart POS as a business platform

Smart POS devices have evolved far beyond the “card swipe machine” stereotype.

They now function as:

  • mini billing systems
  • order-management terminals
  • ERP-integrated retail tools
  • customer-analytics collection points

This shift towards “POS as a business interface” explains why the share of Android smart POS devices has been growing faster than traditional fixed POS.

5. Institutional & government digital initiatives

Banking inclusion, Aadhaar-linked authentication, subsidy distribution, and digitisation of public services have also contributed indirectly to POS growth, particularly in sectors that require identity verification + transaction recording on the same device.

What this means for buyers & procurement teams

  • Scalability & Future Proofing - As payment habits shift (cards, UPI, wallets, contactless), terminals must handle multi-payment modes.
  • Durability & Reliability - Many deployments will be in Indian rural or semi-urban environments with variable conditions.
  • Compliance & Security - Hardware must meet security standards.
  • Integration Capability - Devices should integrate with ERP, inventory, billing or biometric systems.
  • Local Support & After-Sales Service - Critical for multi-location deployments.

What buyers should ask before investing

Question Why It Matters
Is the terminal hybrid (supports card, UPI QR, wallet, NFC, contactless)? Ensures flexibility across payment preferences.
Is the device rugged, suited for Indian store environments? Reduces hardware failure, maintenance cost, downtime.
Can the device integrate with ERP, billing, inventory or biometric systems? Enables future expansion.
Is there a reliable local support & service network? Critical for multi-location deployments.
Does the vendor provide security compliance? Essential for trust and data protection.
Are the total cost of ownership and upgrade path clear? Avoids lock-in and ensures longevity.
